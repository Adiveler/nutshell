: What is Nutshell?

Nutshell is a tool to help you make "expandable explanations", like this one! They can even be :recursive.

Why the heck did I make this? Well, I want to write about technical stuff... but I want it to remain accessible to everyone... but I don't want to have to re-explain stuff over and over... and using links suck.

Unlike links, Nutshell lets you only include the snippet you need, not the whole page. Moreover, instead of being lost in a jungle of new tabs, your reader stays on the same page, not losing the flow of reading. Even if you :interrupt a sentence, an "expanded explanation" recaps the sentence afterwards, so your reader never loses context.

Unlike :Wikipedia's hover-to-preview-article, you can go multiple layers deep without losing flow. Not only that, you can re-use explanations from other websites and authors, even stuff that was written long ago! (For example, here's a snippet from :my pretentious 2014 blog post.) This works because Nutshell doesn't require you write in any new format, just the good ol' headers & paragraphs you're used to.

But wait, there's more! It's not just text & pictures you can embed! You can embed :interactive stuff, :YouTube videos, and â€“ hey, why not â€“ :Wikipedia articles. (That includes :other languages and :Simple Wikipedia, too!)

So if you're writing a blog, a news article, educational material, or code documentation... I hope Nutshell helps you help your readers, by giving them an option to learn just-in-time, always-in-context.

Bite-sized, yet nutritious. Let's get crackin' with Nutshell!

: Tips on writing Nutshells

If you'd like to explain something in a nutshell (ayyyyy there's the title drop!), here's some advice:

  • Show, then tell. Start with concrete examples & pictures, then lay down the abstract definitions.
  • Write your first draft, then cut 10% of it. To do that: get a word count (for example, use wordcounter.net), multiply that by 0.9 to get your new target word count, then cut useless words until you meet your target.
  • This ain't Wikipedia. You can actually use your own voice, not Neutralese.
  • I know recursion is fun, but do show restraint, and :don't :stuff :your :explanations :with :Nutshells :like :this. Only embed what's essential.

: What inspired this?

Once upon a time, back when people actually believed that connecting the world's people would bring forth a new era of empathy and enlightenment (ha ha ha hA HA HAAAAAA--)

...in that time, Ted Nelson, the inventor of hypertext, proposed something called StretchText. The idea was you have an article at a high-level narrative, but could "stretch" sentences into more detail. Then you stretch that detail into more detail, and so on, while everything stays in one continuous story â€“ not a jungle of new tabs.

(See :the original 1967 document for StretchText, and Nelson :showing it in a documentary)

Since then, this idea's been re-discovered a few times. In 2008, a viral website named Telescopic Text let you stretch "I made tea" into a whole short story. In 2018, Wikipedia added a :"hover to preview article" feature. (:more "prior art" examples)

And now, in 2022, I'm giving you Nutshell! My main value-add, compared to previous StretchText-likes, is that you can embed snippets from other websites & authors, even stuff written long ago. This way, we can build upon each others' explanations, standing upon the shoulders of others who are standing upon the shoulders of others who are standing upon the shoulders of... and so on.

Maybe that'll bring about that new era of empathy and enlightenment!

: Open Source
